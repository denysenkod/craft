# The Mom Test — Condensed Framework

Good customer conversations are about their life, not your idea. The moment you mention what you're building, people switch from truth to politeness.

## Three Rules

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea** — never mention your solution until the end (if at all)
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not hypotheticals about the future** — past behavior is the only reliable data
3. **Talk less, listen more** — aim for them to speak 80%+ of the time

## Good vs Bad Questions

**Bad (always gets a "yes"):**
- "Do you think it's a good idea?"
- "Would you buy a product that does X?"
- "How much would you pay for X?"

**Good (reveals real behavior):**
- "How are you dealing with this problem today?"
- "What have you tried before and why did you stop?"
- "Tell me about the last time you [problem area]"
- "What's the hardest part about [doing this thing]?"
- "Walk me through how you handled this last week"
- "What else have you tried?"
- "Where does the money come from for solutions like this?"

A question fails the Mom Test if the answer is always "yes" regardless of whether the business will succeed. The scariest questions (ones you're afraid to ask) usually produce the most useful data.

## Three Types of Bad Data

1. **Compliments** — "That's a great idea!" contains zero information. Deflect: "Thanks — but what are you doing about this today?"
2. **Fluff** — "I usually," "I always," "I would never" are worthless without a specific instance. Anchor: "Can you tell me about a specific time?"
3. **Ideas** — Feature requests disconnected from problems. Dig: "What's driving that? Tell me about the last time you needed something like that"

Symptoms of a bad conversation: you walk away feeling great but have no concrete facts or commitments.

## Commitment & Advancement

Real interest = willingness to invest time, reputation, or money. Every conversation should end with a clear advance or clear rejection. The worst outcome is a zombie lead.

**Commitment currencies:**
- Time: meeting, trial, pilot
- Reputation: intro to boss/colleague, testimonial
- Money: deposit, pre-order, letter of intent

Always know your "ask" before the meeting. A "no" is more valuable than a "maybe."

**Advancing questions:**
- "What's the next step here?"
- "Would you be willing to try a prototype next week?"
- "Who else should I talk to about this?"
- "Could you introduce me to the person who handles the budget?"
- "Can I put you on the early access list?"

## Finding Conversations

Keep it casual — "I'm trying to learn" beats "I'm doing customer research." Formal interview framing triggers performance mode.

- Cold outreach: keep short, lead with their expertise, don't pitch
- Warm intros: one good advisor can open dozens of doors
- Go where your customers already gather (events, communities)
- "I'm researching how [industry] handles [problem] — could I learn from your experience?"

## Processing & Learning

- Take notes during or immediately after — never rely on memory
- Separate facts (what they said/did) from interpretations (what you think it means)
- Share raw notes with team, not filtered summaries
- Update three beliefs: the problem, the customer segment, the solution
- When conversations start repeating, stop talking and start building

## Quick Diagnostic

| Check | If No |
|-------|-------|
| Did it focus on their life/past behavior? | You pitched instead of learning |
| Did you get concrete facts about what they've done? | You collected opinions |
| Did they give a commitment (time/reputation/money)? | Possible zombie lead |
| Did they do most of the talking? | You talked too much |
| Did you learn something that could change what you're building? | You asked safe questions |
| Can you summarize key facts (not opinions)? | Your notes need work |

## Key Patterns

- "Tell me about the last time you..."
- "What happened next?"
- "How are you dealing with that currently?"
- "Can you walk me through your process?"
- "When you say you'd 'definitely' use this, what would you stop using?"
- "Thanks, but to make sure I'm not wasting your time — what does your current process look like?"
